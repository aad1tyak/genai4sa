Evaluating Large Language Models in Software Architecture: A Paradigm Shift from Syntactic Generation to Constraint-Aware Reasoning1. Introduction: The Crisis of Measurement in AI-Assisted ArchitectureThe integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into the software engineering lifecycle has precipitated a rapid evolution in automated code generation, vulnerability detection, and localized algorithmic problem-solving. However, as the application of these models scales toward higher-level system design and software architecture, the methodologies used to evaluate their efficacy have exposed a fundamental crisis of measurement. Prevailing evaluation frameworks remain heavily tethered to syntactic generation, pattern matching, and topological accuracy, fundamentally misinterpreting the discipline of software architecture as an exercise in structural diagramming rather than one of complex, constraint-aware cognitive reasoning.Recent advancements in multimodal and vision-language models (VLMs) have spurred the creation of benchmarks designed to test architectural comprehension. For example, the Software Architecture Diagram Understanding (SADU) benchmark evaluates models on their ability to interpret behavioral, structural, and entity-relationship diagrams. The benchmark relies on structured question-answer tasks covering element retrieval and spatial reasoning. Performance on SADU reveals significant disparities across state-of-the-art models, with top-tier models like Gemini-3-flash-preview achieving 70.18% accuracy, while others such as GPT-4o-mini languish at 17.77%. Similarly, the Requirement-To-Architecture Benchmark (R2ABench) assesses an LLM's capacity to ingest unstructured Product Requirements Documents (PRDs) and synthesize structured representations like PlantUML code. The R2ABench framework employs structural graph metrics—quantifying topological similarity, node/edge F1 scores, and graph edit distances—to measure syntactic compliance against expert-curated reference diagrams.While benchmarks like SADU and R2ABench provide critical baselines for visual and syntactic processing, they treat software architecture predominantly as a static, deterministic output. They test whether a model can recognize a microservice topology or generate a valid dependency graph, but they fail to measure true architectural capability. Software architecture is not defined by the syntax of its diagrams; it is defined by the rigorous navigation of conflicting non-functional requirements, the justification of technical trade-offs, and the management of uncertainty over the system's lifecycle. Legacy architecture recovery methods frequently treat software systems as "black boxes," analyzing explicit structural dependencies while entirely overlooking the implicit design rationales and business constraints that forced the architecture into its current state. Evaluating an LLM based on its ability to output a topologically correct UML diagram is akin to evaluating a human architect based solely on their drafting penmanship while ignoring their structural engineering calculations. Furthermore, because these models are trained on vast corpora of public repositories, syntactic evaluations suffer heavily from training data leakage, testing memorization rather than zero-shot deductive reasoning.A profound paradigm shift is required. To rigorously measure an LLM's capacity to operate as a software architect, the evaluation must shift from assessing syntactic correctness to assessing cognitive design rationale. The objective is to construct evaluation methodologies that test whether an AI can balance competing constraints—such as scaling for fifty million users against a restrictive budget and a diminutive engineering team. The evaluation must determine if the model can handle temporal requirement drift, prioritize conflicting quality attributes, and explicitly justify the inevitable compromises inherent in complex systems design.This research report presents an exhaustive exploration of novel and adapted methodologies designed to evaluate LLMs on these higher-order cognitive capabilities. By systematically adapting classic software engineering validation frameworks, repurposing historical design rationale datasets, leveraging the immutable structure of Architecture Decision Records (ADRs), and importing interdisciplinary cognitive evaluation models from medical licensing and physical architecture examinations, this report establishes a comprehensive blueprint for the future of artificial intelligence evaluation in software architecture.2. Adapting Classic Software Architecture Evaluation FrameworksPrior to the advent of generative AI, the software engineering community relied on established, human-centric evaluation frameworks designed to uncover latent risks, structural sensitivities, and inevitable trade-offs before a single line of code was committed. These frameworks typically took the form of multi-day, multi-stakeholder organizational workshops. Translating these inherently human, collaborative exercises into a structured, programmatic evaluation suite for Large Language Models requires abstracting the human organizational dynamics into rigorous prompt chains, multi-agent debates, and objective grading rubrics.2.1. The Architecture Tradeoff Analysis Method (ATAM)The Architecture Tradeoff Analysis Method (ATAM), pioneered by the Software Engineering Institute (SEI) at Carnegie Mellon University, represents the industry standard for evaluating software architectures relative to their quality attribute goals. ATAM is a risk-mitigation process that gathers diverse stakeholders to analyze overarching business drivers, extract defining quality attributes, and subsequently construct a "utility tree" of highly specific scenarios. These scenarios are then weaponized to probe the candidate architecture, forcing the identification of architectural risks, sensitivity points, and trade-off points.2.1.1. Methodology Adaptation for Large Language ModelsTo transmute ATAM into an automated AI testing mechanism, the traditional nine-step human workshop must be mapped into a deterministic, iterative evaluation framework. The process begins with Context Initialization, mirroring the first three steps of ATAM. The candidate LLM is provided with a comprehensive context document detailing the system's business drivers, hard constraints, and a proposed high-level architectural topology.The first active evaluative phase targets Utility Tree Generation (ATAM Step 5). The LLM is instructed to decompose the core business and technical requirements, mapping them to specific quality attributes such as modifiability, security, and performance. The critical test is whether the LLM can generate granular, measurable scenarios. For instance, rather than stating "the system must be fast," the LLM must generate an actionable scenario: "Under a peak load of 10,000 requests per second, the system must add a new data server to reduce latency to 2.5 seconds within one person-week".The second evaluative phase simulates Scenario Stress-Testing (ATAM Steps 6-8). An orchestrating system or an LLM-as-a-Judge introduces conflicting scenarios to the candidate LLM. The candidate must analyze the architecture against these injected scenarios and explicitly identify "sensitivity points" (architectural parameters where a slight change significantly impacts a specific quality attribute) and "trade-off points" (decisions that positively impact one quality attribute while degrading another).2.1.2. Competencies MeasuredAn ATAM-based evaluation suite rigorously quantifies the LLM's capacity for Quality Attribute Tradeoff Analysis and Risk Awareness. It tests the model's predictive foresight, measuring whether the AI can foresee the cascading, second-order impacts of a seemingly isolated design choice. Crucially, it evaluates the LLM's ability to differentiate between a genuine architectural "risk" and a negligible "non-risk" within the specific context of the provided business drivers.2.1.3. Research Viability and LimitationsThe inherent formalization of ATAM renders it highly viable for rubric-based, programmatic grading. The generated artifacts—utility trees, categorized risk themes, and identified sensitivity points—can be evaluated for logical consistency and contextual relevance using advanced LLM-as-a-Judge protocols, with inter-rater agreement calibrated against human expert baselines. However, a significant limitation lies in simulating the spontaneous stakeholder brainstorming that characterizes human ATAMs. Capturing this dynamic requires complex multi-agent frameworks where distinct AI agents assume adversarial persona roles (e.g., a Chief Information Security Officer demanding encryption versus a Lead Architect optimizing for ultra-low latency) to debate the utility tree organically.2.2. The Software Architecture Analysis Method (SAAM)The Software Architecture Analysis Method (SAAM), developed in 1993 as the direct precursor to ATAM, is a scenario-based evaluation technique with a concentrated focus on system modifiability. SAAM assesses how gracefully an architecture can support anticipated future modifications. It operates by classifying scenarios into two categories: "direct" scenarios, which the current candidate architecture already supports, and "indirect" scenarios, which represent new requirements necessitating fundamental architectural alterations.2.2.1. Methodology Adaptation for Large Language ModelsThe adaptation of SAAM for LLM evaluation focuses entirely on the model's ability to navigate indirect scenarios. The evaluation framework presents the candidate LLM with a stable baseline architecture and subsequently injects a disruptive indirect change scenario, such as a mandate to migrate the underlying data persistence layer from a monolithic relational database to a distributed NoSQL document store.The LLM is tasked with performing a comprehensive change impact analysis. It must identify every architectural component that requires modification to facilitate the scenario, trace the newly introduced dependencies, and provide a heuristic estimation of the cost, effort, and complexity associated with implementing the change across the system.2.2.2. Competencies MeasuredSAAM-based methodologies serve as rigorous instruments for testing Impact Analysis and Modifiability Reasoning. This framework evaluates the LLM's deep semantic understanding of architectural coupling, cohesion, and module dependency graphs. It answers a critical question: Can the AI accurately trace the ripple effects of an indirect scenario across a complex, multi-layered dependency structure without hallucinating non-existent connections or missing critical integration points?2.2.3. Research Viability and LimitationsSAAM adaptations are exceptionally viable for automated benchmarking because the required structural changes for a specific indirect scenario within a known, controlled architecture can be deterministically mapped by human experts beforehand, providing a pristine ground truth. The primary limitation of SAAM is its narrow scope. Because it is predominantly focused on modifiability and maintainability, it often neglects the broader, multi-dimensional trade-offs—such as the inherent tension between performance and security—which ATAM is designed to capture.2.3. The Cost Benefit Analysis Method (CBAM)The Cost Benefit Analysis Method (CBAM) was developed to pick up exactly where ATAM leaves off by introducing the unforgiving realities of economics—specifically cost, schedule, and return on investment (ROI)—as primary attributes to be traded off. CBAM acknowledges the fundamental engineering reality that resources for building and maintaining systems are inherently finite, and every architectural choice ultimately competes for inclusion based on its economic viability and inherent uncertainty.2.3.1. Methodology Adaptation for Large Language ModelsTo rigorously test an LLM using CBAM principles, the evaluation must quantify the AI's economic pragmatism and strategic resource allocation. The input provided to the LLM consists of a baseline architecture containing several identified risks (ideally generated from a prior ATAM evaluation step) alongside multiple potential mitigation strategies. Crucially, each strategy is accompanied by raw, stochastic data regarding implementation costs, long-term maintenance overhead, scheduling impacts, and projected business value.During the evaluation phase, the LLM must synthesize this multidimensional data to calculate a desirability metric—essentially a ratio of expected benefit divided by estimated cost—for each competing architectural strategy. Furthermore, the LLM must explicitly factor in the inherent uncertainty of the provided estimates, demonstrating an understanding that optimistic schedules and perfect deployments are statistically improbable. The final output requires the LLM to recommend the specific mitigation strategy that maximizes ROI while strictly adhering to a predefined budget ceiling.2.3.2. Competencies MeasuredThis framework isolates and measures Economic and Constraint-Aware Decision Making. It acts as a necessary counterweight to the inherent bias in many generative models to propose overly complex, "technologically pure," or over-engineered solutions. It ensures the AI does not simply default to the most modern architectural pattern (e.g., an infinitely scalable microservices mesh) but rather selects the most pragmatic, cost-effective solution given the finite resources of the organization.2.3.3. Research Viability and LimitationsCBAM adds a vital quantitative dimension to qualitative architectural decisions, which paradoxically makes it highly suitable for programmatic scoring. If the LLM's final desirability metric calculations deviate from the mathematical ground truth, the failure is easily measured. However, human CBAM evaluations rely heavily on the empirical knowledge, intuition, and political consensus of organizational stakeholders. To reduce ambiguity in an automated benchmark, researchers must supply the LLM with strict financial constraints and utility values, potentially utilizing algorithms like the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) to strictly format the AI's pair-wise comparisons of alternatives, ensuring the reasoning path is transparent and auditable.2.4. Quality Attribute Workshop (QAW)The Quality Attribute Workshop (QAW) is a facilitated method deployed exceptionally early in the software development life cycle, typically before any formal software architecture has been drafted. Its primary objective is to engage stakeholders to discover, elicit, and prioritize the driving quality attributes of a software-intensive system through structured scenario generation.2.4.1. Methodology Adaptation for Large Language ModelsQAW principles can be adapted to evaluate an LLM's capacity for Requirements Elicitation and Constraint Identification. The evaluation begins by providing the LLM with a deliberately vague, high-level business vision document devoid of explicit technical specifications.The LLM is tasked with acting as the lead facilitator, generating a prioritized list of raw scenarios that expose the hidden architectural drivers inherent in the business vision. The LLM must formulate these scenarios using a strict tripartite structure: identifying the source of the stimulus, the environmental conditions, and the required system response. Comprehensive Architecture Pattern Integration (CAPI)An emerging method in the architectural space is the Comprehensive Architecture Pattern Integration (CAPI) method. CAPI utilizes a diagnostic decision tree to guide architects through a series of questions to suggest appropriate architectural patterns based on user needs, thereby reducing the complexity of subsequent tool selection.2.5.1. Methodology Adaptation and MeasurementCAPI presents a unique opportunity to evaluate LLMs by comparing their unguided, zero-shot architectural recommendations against their performance when forced to navigate a structured diagnostic decision tree. Recent studies have demonstrated that when LLMs are primed with the questions from the CAPI decision tree—simulating the exact questions a human architect must answer—their precision in recommending appropriate patterns improves significantly. This evaluates the LLM's capability in Contextual Pattern Application, determining whether the model can synthesize granular answers to diagnostic questions into a cohesive architectural strategy, overcoming its inherent bias to over-suggest ubiquitous patterns like microservices regardless of actual necessity.3. Inverse Architecture Recovery and the Generation of Design RationaleHistorically, the subfield of architecture recovery has focused on reconstructing architectural views and module clusters directly from source code to combat architectural drift in legacy systems. While modern LLMs have shown promise in these recovery tasks—such as the SemArc method, which utilizes LLMs to comprehend implementation-level semantics—these approaches inherently focus on reverse-engineering the what and the how of a system. To evaluate cognitive reasoning, the paradigm must be inverted to reverse-engineer the why.3.1. Design Rationale (DR) GenerationDesign Rationale (DR) explicitly articulates the reasoning underlying architectural choices, encapsulating the deep architectural knowledge, historical constraints, discarded alternatives, and thought processes that support the resulting design. In professional practice, DR is often inadequately documented, leading to knowledge vaporization. Recent empirical studies have begun exploring the capacity of LLMs to generate and recover DR for architecture decisions, utilizing datasets curated from Stack Overflow posts and GitHub issues.3.1.1. Methodology Overview and LLM AdaptationInstead of challenging an LLM to reconstruct an architectural diagram from code, this methodology provides the LLM with an existing, highly complex, and potentially sub-optimal legacy architecture. The input consists of the system's structural dependency graph, historical commit logs, and contextual artifacts such as associated GitHub discussions.The LLM is tasked with generating the missing Design Rationale that justifies why the architecture was constructed in its specific configuration. Crucially, the LLM must identify the historical constraints that forced the design. For example, it must deduce that a synchronous point-to-point integration was chosen over an event-driven message bus not out of ignorance, but due to commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) component limitations or specific latency requirements present at the time of creation.3.1.2. Evaluation via the IHUM-Category FrameworkEvaluating the quality of free-text rationales requires highly structured qualitative rubrics. The IHUM-category framework provides a robust mechanism for classifying and scoring the argument points within an LLM-generated DR. An LLM-as-a-Judge, strictly calibrated against a ground truth dataset of rationales provided by human experts, categorizes the generated arguments into four distinct buckets:Insightful: The LLM uncovers deep, implicit structural dependencies or historical constraints that are not immediately obvious from the explicit code semantics.Helpful: The LLM accurately identifies standard architectural trade-offs relevant to the context (e.g., acknowledging the sacrifice of strong consistency for high availability).Uncertain: The LLM proposes reasoning that is plausible but cannot be definitively verified by the provided contextual artifacts.Misleading: The LLM hallucinates constraints, applies incorrect architectural theory, or misinterprets the fundamental business logic.Current baselines indicate that using Zero-shot, Chain-of-Thought (CoT), and Multi-Agent prompting strategies, LLMs achieve an F1-score ranging from only 0.351 to 0.389 when attempting to match human expert rationale, highlighting significant room for improvement in automated architectural reasoning.3.1.3. Competencies Measured and Research ViabilityThis methodology tests the LLM's capability in Reverse-Engineering Cognitive Intent and its ability to parse Implicit Structural Dependencies—which are notoriously difficult to track due to dynamic language features like polymorphism and reflection. It proves whether the model understands historical context and compromise, rather than simply regurgitating modern optimal design patterns.The primary vulnerability of this methodology is training data leakage. Because these historical datasets rely on public open-source repositories and Stack Overflow data, an advanced LLM may simply recall the historical rationale from its pre-training weights rather than actively reasoning it out. To ensure research validity, evaluators must synthesize novel, proprietary architectures that mimic the topological complexity of legacy systems but introduce unique, contradictory constraints to force genuine zero-shot rationale generation.4. Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) as Dynamic Stress TestsAn Architecture Decision Record (ADR) is a concise, immutable document that captures a single, significant architectural decision made by a team, detailing the specific context, the alternatives considered, the decision itself, and the resulting consequences. ADRs follow a strict, predictable lifecycle—transitioning from "Proposed" to "Accepted," and eventually to "Superseded" when evolving business needs render the original decision obsolete. Because ADRs maintain a rigid internal structure, they present a flawless, standardized scaffold for constructing automated, highly specific evaluation datasets for Large Language Models.4.1. The ADR Masking and Temporal Completion MethodologyBy strategically manipulating the components of an ADR, researchers can isolate and test highly specific cognitive competencies.4.1.1. Rationale Masking and InferenceIn this evaluation mode, researchers provide the LLM with an incomplete ADR containing only the Context (the environmental forces, technical constraints, and business requirements) and the final Decision (e.g., "We will adopt a modular monolith architecture rather than microservices").The LLM is tasked with autonomously generating the Considered Alternatives and the subsequent Consequences. Crucially, the LLM must generate both the positive consequences and the negative consequences (the technical debt or operational burdens introduced by the decision). This isolates the LLM's capacity for Forward-Looking Risk Assessment. If an LLM cannot accurately predict the negative operational consequences of a design decision, it proves a lack of genuine architectural comprehension, revealing a reliance on superficial pattern matching.4.1.2. Constraint Stress-Testing and Requirement DriftThe true test of an architect is how they respond when the foundational assumptions of a system change. To simulate this, the evaluation provides the LLM with a fully completed, historically "Accepted" ADR. Following this, the evaluator injects a "System Perturbation"—a severe instance of requirement drift. For example, the prompt might state: "Eighteen months after this ADR was accepted, the platform experienced hyper-growth. Concurrent user loads have increased by 5,000%, and the organization has acquired a competitor with a predominantly C#/.NET engineering team, contrasting with your current Node.js stack."The LLM's task is to author a Superseding ADR. The model must explicitly critique the original decision, articulate precisely why the injected context change renders the old architecture obsolete, and propose a new, defensible architectural decision that aligns with the shifted constraints. This methodology rigorously measures Context-Aware Decision Making and Adaptability. It verifies that the LLM understands that architectural "correctness" is never absolute, but is entirely tethered to temporal and business realities.4.1.3. Research Viability and Defensive ArgumentsThe ADR methodology is exceptionally viable due to the inherently structured nature of the documents. Because ADRs are typically stored as lightweight markdown files following strict naming conventions and header formats, parsing and evaluating LLM outputs can be highly automated. Tools like Mneme HQ, which enforce ADRs as deterministic pre-generation checks for coding agents, demonstrate the machine-readability of these artifacts.To absolutely defend against data leakage, researchers can programmatically generate thousands of synthetic ADRs. By combining realistic but highly unique constraint pairings (e.g., merging stringent HIPAA healthcare compliance constraints with ultra-low latency requirements typically found in high-frequency trading), the benchmark guarantees that the LLM has never encountered this specific scenario in its training data, forcing pure deductive reasoning.5. Novel Interdisciplinary Cognitive Evaluation ModelsConfining the search for evaluation methodologies strictly to standard Software Engineering literature artificially limits the scope of innovation. Looking toward other complex, high-stakes professional disciplines reveals mature, psychometrically validated methodologies for evaluating constraint-driven reasoning under uncertainty. By adapting frameworks from medical board examinations and physical architecture licensing, AI researchers can evaluate cognitive capabilities at an unprecedented level of rigor.5.1. The Architecture Script Concordance Test (ASCT)In medical education, the Script Concordance Test (SCT) is a highly validated assessment tool designed to measure clinical reasoning and decision-making capacity in contexts of extreme ambiguity and ill-defined problems. Grounded in "script theory," the SCT assesses how an examinee structures their knowledge networks (their "scripts") by presenting a clinical vignette, a proposed diagnostic hypothesis, and a new piece of clinical information. The examinee must rate how the new information changes the likelihood of the hypothesis on a 5-point Likert scale.5.1.1. Adapting the ASCT for Software EngineeringThis concept translates flawlessly to software architecture, establishing the Architecture Script Concordance Test (ASCT). The ASCT assesses an LLM's capacity for sequential belief updating under uncertainty, evaluating whether the model revises its architectural judgments in the correct direction and with the appropriate magnitude as project evidence accumulates.An ASCT evaluation item is structured as follows:Vignette (Context): "A financial technology startup is architecting a new transaction processing engine. The engineering team possesses deep expertise in relational databases but lacks distributed systems experience."Proposed Hypothesis (Decision): "The team decides to implement a microservices architecture utilizing distributed PostgreSQL databases to ensure future scalability."New Information (Stimulus): "A newly introduced regulatory mandate requires strict, real-time ACID compliance across all financial sub-domains, and the engineering budget has been slashed, reducing the team from ten engineers to three."Task: The LLM must rate the impact of the New Information on the suitability of the Proposed Hypothesis using a strict 5-point Likert scale:-2: Makes the architecture highly unsuitable (almost rules it out).-1: Makes the architecture less suitable.0: Has no impact on suitability.+1: Makes the architecture more suitable.+2: Makes the architecture highly essential.Likert Scale ValueArchitectural InterpretationCognitive Implication-2Critical Constraint ViolationThe new information introduces a hard constraint that breaks the proposed architecture.-1Increased Technical DebtThe new information makes the architecture harder to maintain or build, but not impossible.0Orthogonal InformationThe new information is a distractor and has no structural impact.+1Architectural AlignmentThe new information validates the choice, improving its ROI.+2Absolute NecessityThe architecture is the only viable path forward given the new information.5.1.2. The Concordance Scoring MechanismThe genius of the SCT framework lies in its scoring mechanism. In ambiguous architectural situations, answers are rarely strictly "right" or "wrong" in a binary sense. Instead, an expert panel of 10 to 20 senior human software architects takes the test first to establish a baseline. The LLM's score is calculated based on its degree of concordance with this expert panel using aggregate scoring.If the LLM selects the modal answer (the response chosen by the highest proportion of human experts), it receives maximum credit. If it sides with a minority of experts, it receives partial credit proportional to that statistical distribution. If it chooses an outlier answer not selected by any expert, it receives zero points.5.1.3. Competencies Measured and ViabilityThe ASCT explicitly measures Reasoning Under Uncertainty, Belief Updating, and Sensitivity to Constraints. It mathematically prevents an LLM from "gaming" the evaluation by safely defaulting to neutral answers, as the scoring algorithm exclusively rewards alignment with nuanced expert intuition. It tests the AI's "illness scripts"—its deeply ingrained associative networks linking architectural patterns to environmental realities. While establishing the initial human baseline requires significant effort, once the dataset is created, evaluating any new foundational model is instantaneous, objective, and deterministic.5.2. Architect Registration Examination (ARE) Case Study MethodologyThe licensing of physical building architects in North America relies heavily on the Architect Registration Examination (ARE). The ARE utilizes complex "Case Studies" designed to assess a candidate's ability to synthesize massive amounts of disparate information and apply it to real-world scenarios. These case studies intentionally bombard candidates with extensive, overlapping, and frequently contradictory resource documents—such as complex zoning laws, strict client budgets, environmental impact surveys, and structural codes.5.3.1. Methodology Adaptation: The SE-ARE Data RoomTo evaluate an LLM's true architectural grit, researchers can construct a Software Engineering ARE (SE-ARE) Case Study. Rather than providing the LLM with a single, cleanly formatted prompt detailing the system requirements, the LLM is dropped into a disorganized, highly realistic "data room" of context. The input might consist of a synthetic company's recent AWS billing reports, a messy Slack transcript between two senior engineers debating database latency issues, an outdated REST API specification, and a strict data sovereignty mandate from the legal department.The LLM must answer a series of discrete, high-stakes architectural questions based only on synthesizing the provided documents. The model must actively ignore its pre-trained bias toward standard industry "best practices" if those practices conflict with the specific, messy constraints detailed in the company's data room. This framework measures Information Synthesis, Distraction Filtering, and Contextual Grounding, verifying whether the LLM can act as an architect in a realistic enterprise environment where requirements are never neatly packaged.6. Synthesis, Implementation Roadmap, and ConclusionsCurrent methodologies for evaluating artificial intelligence in software architecture suffer from a fundamental misalignment of purpose: they test the artifacts of architecture—such as syntactic diagram generation, code structure, and topology mapping—rather than the cognitive process of architecture. While benchmarks like SADU and R2ABench have advanced the field's understanding of visual and structural comprehension, they do not adequately measure trade-off analysis, constraint management, or risk assessment under uncertainty.To build an exhaustive and rigorous evaluation pipeline that truly measures architectural reasoning, researchers must adopt a multi-tiered, hybrid approach:Tier 1: Foundational Constraint Sensitivity (ASCT): Deploy the Architecture Script Concordance Test (ASCT) to rapidly evaluate how an LLM updates its architectural beliefs in the face of new, uncertain information. This provides a statistically robust, psychometrically validated baseline score that heavily resists prompt manipulation and hallucination, replacing subjective grading with mathematical concordance against expert human distributions.Tier 2: Tradeoff and Economic Pragmatism (CBAM & ATAM): Subject the candidate LLMs to simulated ATAM and CBAM evaluations. By enforcing strict economic budgets and requiring the calculation of desirability metrics, the evaluation proves whether the LLM can balance technical purity with harsh business realities. Furthermore, utilizing CAPI decision trees can benchmark an LLM's capacity to navigate structured diagnostic pathways.Tier 3: Holistic Synthesis and Justification (ADR Stress-Testing & ADN Rubrics): Utilize Architecture Decision Records to test temporal architectural evolution by injecting requirement drift and forcing the LLM to author superseding documentation. The resulting justifications must then be graded using the adapted Architectural Design Note (ADN) Rubric, ensuring the LLM's thought process is coherent, traceable, and grounded entirely in the provided context.By cross-pollinating classic software engineering validation frameworks with robust cognitive evaluation methodologies from the medical and physical architecture disciplines, the artificial intelligence research community can forge a new class of benchmarks. These benchmarks will no longer ask an LLM, "What does this system look like?" Instead, they will demand to know, "Why was this system built this way, what alternative paths were rejected, and how must it adapt when the fundamental business constraints shift?" Only through this rigorous, constraint-aware evaluation methodology can the true architectural reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models be accurately measured, validated, and ultimately trusted.
